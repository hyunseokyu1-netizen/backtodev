We just want it to be good.

If it's delicious, it's delicious. Just do it...

We always preface our sentences with... than you think. at the beginning of a sentence.

It's more delicious than I thought, I'm better than I thought... etc...

Even if you watch Korean broadcasts, many people use the word "more than I thought" a lot.

In English, we don't use the word more than I thought. But why is it that only Korean people use the word more than you think?

When we talk about something, there is always a doubt that what if others disagree with my opinion? when we talk about something.

That's why we use the word 'thought than' before expressing our opinion in a defensive manner.

In Korea, humility is seen as a virtue, and people don't like it when you show off even if you're good, so I think I have to be passive when I speak... when I praise.

So, even when praising someone... I say things like, "You did better than I thought," "It's tastier than I thought," "It's different than I thought," and so on.

Just, good job, delicious. You're running well. . I wonder if I should add the word 'better than I thought' in front of it to lower the intensity of the praise.


I wish we were more generous with our praise, and it would be nice to be able to accurately describe good as good.
